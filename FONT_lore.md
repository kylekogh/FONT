## PILLAR 1 — THE SPIRITS & THE BOND *(building now)*

The heart of the game. Fixed physics, open specifics. These are the questions that need answers. Kyle feeds the idea; Claude shapes it.

### 1.1 — What IS a spirit? ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: A spirit is power given form — your Spark manifested. Everyone is born with a Spark that pulls them toward one fundamental law of the universe. As varied as people exist, that's how many Sparks there are. A Spark leaning toward gravity might manifest as pure vectors of push and pull — or as making things float. The more creative the individual, the more expressive the manifestation. CRITICAL: you don't "get" a Spark of gravity. It's natural inclination, Avatar-style — not hardcoded "you are this."

**Fleshed:**

A spirit is your Spark made real. Not a thing you find and put on — a thing you *grow*, the way a body grows around a heartbeat.

Every person is born with a Spark: a pull, faint and specific, toward one of the fundamental laws the universe runs on. Gravity. Combustion. Decay. Momentum. Sound. There are as many Sparks as there are people, because the law you lean toward isn't assigned — it's *inclination*, the way some people are born reaching for music and others for numbers. Two people can both lean toward gravity and never manifest the same power. One becomes vectors of force, raw push and pull you can feel compress the air. The other makes the world go weightless around them. Same law, different person, different spirit.

This is the part that matters: **the Spark is a direction, not a destiny.** Nobody is "a gravity user." Somebody *leans* toward gravity, and what they build from that lean is theirs alone. The more imagination a person brings, the stranger and more expressive the manifestation gets. A literal mind makes a literal power. A creative one makes something nobody's seen before. The spirit is the shape your Spark takes when you've spent your life pulling on that one thread of the universe — and it keeps taking new shape as long as you keep pulling.

*[Filter check: this serves writing directly — "creativity = expressiveness" means the player's imagination is mechanically rewarded, which is the whole point of FONT as a writing engine. Coheres with the redesign's "power given form." Locked-adjacent — say if the prose drifts from your intent.]*

### 1.2 — What is bonding, actually? ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: The Spark is like a chakra center — the spiritual heart of an individual. It houses spirits and enables their powers. A heart, or a nervous system, for power. Bonding is spiritual in nature but physical in manifestation (the powers) — which is WHY a break is so devastating or empowering. Bonding is a relationship: lifelong, sometimes chosen (e.g. in dungeons), but usually the spirit finds the individual in their moment of awakening. Being bonded feels like ALWAYS HAVING SOMEONE WATCHING YOUR BACK — two people vs a group, standing back to back, knowing they're going out swinging. Capture that feeling.

**Fleshed:**

The Spark sits at the center of a person the way a heart does — not an organ you could point to on a chart, but the place power lives and moves out from. Think of it as the spiritual heart and the nervous system of what you can do: the spirit houses there, and from there its power runs out into your hands, your body, the world.

That dual nature is the whole thing. Bonding is *spiritual* — a relationship between two wills — but it manifests *physically*, as force and fire and weight you can touch. The bond lives in the soul and speaks through the body. Which is exactly why breaking one is catastrophic or transcendent and never neutral: you're not losing a tool, you're losing something wired into the core of you.

Bonding is a relationship, and like any real one it has a beginning. Usually the spirit finds you — at your awakening, the moment something cracks you open wide enough. Sometimes you choose it, the way a delver claims a wild spirit from the deep. Either way, once it's done, you're not alone in your own skin anymore.

And that's what it *feels* like — the thing every bonded person knows and most never have to put into words. **It's the feeling of someone at your back.** Two of you against a room that wants you gone, standing spine to spine, and the absolute certainty that whatever comes, you're going out swinging *together*. Not safety. Not comfort. The specific, spine-straightening steadiness of knowing the one direction you can't watch is already covered. That's the bond. That's what people are terrified to lose.

*[Filter check: the back-to-back image is the emotional core — built the section to land on it. The "spiritual cause, physical effect" framing is load-bearing: it's the in-world reason breaks matter, which sets up 1.3. Say if "chakra center" should stay as in-world vocabulary or just inform the prose.]*

### 1.3 — Why can the bond break? ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: Most people are used to always having some accompanying spirit. To lose it is devastating. NEGATIVE break: the person becomes hardened, lost, scared, or scarred. They may dominate their next spirit — refusing it autonomy for greater power but less versatility. Or struggle to trust, so the next bond starts weak with latent power that can bloom later. POSITIVE break: a mutual departure makes the next bonding an easy transition; a spirit that sacrifices itself in the moment leaves the user grieving and more protective of future partners. (Examples, not a finite list.) A bond CAN repair — at the moment of breaking, they can come back together. If they do, it's an epic moment, a real Gurren Lagann swing.

**Fleshed:**

Almost everyone carries a spirit their whole life — even the mundane ones, the quiet domestic spirits most people never push past. To have that *taken*, to feel the back you trusted suddenly bare — there's no ordinary word for it. It's not grief and it's not injury. It's both, in the one place you can't reach.

**A negative break leaves a mark on what comes after.** The person comes out hardened, or lost, or frightened in a way that doesn't fully heal. And the next bond carries the shape of the wound. Some survivors *dominate* the spirit they bond next — clamp down, refuse it any autonomy, trade the spirit's voice for raw obedient power. More force, less range; a louder gun that only fires one way. Others can't trust at all, and the new bond starts thin and cold — except that what's withheld doesn't vanish. It sits latent, and if trust ever comes, it blooms into something the person never could have reached the easy way. *(These are shapes a break can take, not the only ones.)*

**A positive break is rarer and gentler.** Two wills that part on mutual terms — the relationship simply ran its course — leave the person open, and the next bonding is an easy, almost familiar transition. Hardest and most luminous of all: a spirit that *chooses* to spend itself, that burns out covering you in the one moment that mattered. The person who survives that carries the grief like a coal, and becomes fiercely, permanently protective of whatever they bond next. They know exactly what a partner can cost. They never forget it.

And here is the thing that makes it a Gurren Lagann game and not a tragedy: **a breaking bond can catch.** In the very instant of severance — the worst second of a person's life — the two of them can choose each other again. Reach back across the break and hold. It almost never happens. The odds are against it, the cost is everything, and when it lands it is the loudest, most defiant moment the game has: not a power-up earned by grinding but a *refusal*, two wills screaming *no, not like this* and meaning it hard enough to make it true.

*[Filter check: maps cleanly onto the Hollow class and the boss-defeat outcome tree (surge/sacrifice/shatter). The repair-at-the-moment is the glory ceiling from the tone filter — kept it rare and costly so it stays earned. The "examples not finite" instruction is preserved as an explicit in-text caveat.]*

### 1.4 — The Bleed ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: It's a narrative/thematic device — something to link the two sides of the superhero thing. Sometimes the world collides because this is one city/country, and a super-level individual will plausibly see patterns or the same people more than once in their area. Sometimes it's something bigger — toward the climax of arcs, links become more frequent, leading to something larger. Not always how a climax goes, but a possibility.

**Fleshed:**

The Bleed isn't a power. It's the name for a truth about living two lives in one city: **they don't actually stay separate.**

Sora is one place. A person who fights in the dark and works in the daylight is moving through the same streets, the same districts, the same handful of thousand faces, twice — once masked, once not. Sooner or later the daylight life and the night life touch. The witness from last night turns out to be the neighbour you nod to every morning. The person you're protecting in your civilian hours is tangled in the thing you're hunting after dark. Most of the time it's just the math of a closed city: cross enough paths and they start to overlap. That's the everyday Bleed, and a bonded person learns to dread the small coincidence.

But sometimes it's more than math. As a story builds toward its peak, the collisions come faster and stop feeling like chance. The same name keeps surfacing. The threads start *pointing* somewhere. It isn't always how a climax arrives — but when the Bleed thickens, when the two lives start crashing into each other on every turn, something larger is usually waking up underneath. The Reckoning is where you ask the question every shift: *did the threads touch tonight?* And when the answer keeps coming back yes, you're no longer living two lives that happen to share a city. You're living one story that's been pretending to be two.

*[Filter check: kept it a thematic/narrative device per your call, NOT a metaphysical force — it explains the Reckoning mechanic without over-canonizing. The arc-climax escalation hooks into the existing arc-weighting in the Reckoning oracle. Deliberately leaves "something larger" open — that's a player-fills-the-blank by design.]*

### 1.5 — Sensory signature of the bond ✨ + ⚠️ CANON CHANGE
> KYLE'S IDEA: An active Spark feels like being full of energy — coffee without the racing heart. Vitality. Sensing another bonded person ISN'T A THING — drop it. You CAN sense manifested spirits in the dungeon: a pulling pressure in the core. Most people have SOME spirit (mundane ones); fewer have the Spark-training/desire/capacity for hero/villain/anti-hero/antagonist stuff. Think MHA quirks — everyone has one, not everyone is a hero. Spirit VOICE varies with the spirit and links to personality: a calm person tends toward a calm spirit (feels like a gentle breeze, sounds like a calm teacher); a fiery personality runs hot-blooded (feels like an engine ready to roar, speaks with passion — not loud, passion). Someone calm can end up with a hot-blooded spirit because deep down that's who they really are and they don't know it yet. (Examples, not finite.)

**Fleshed:**

**An active Spark feels like vitality.** Not adrenaline, not a racing heart — the clean version of that. Like good coffee without the jitter: full, awake, lit from the inside, every system running a little hotter and steadier than a person without a Spark ever feels. Bonded people walk through the world slightly more *there* than everyone else, and most of them stop noticing it the way you stop noticing your own pulse.

**You can feel a manifested spirit in the dungeon** — the loose, hostile ones, the ones that aren't bonded to anyone. It registers as a pulling pressure in the core, low and physical, a tug behind the sternum that says *something powered is near and it isn't on your side.* That's the body's early warning down in the deep.

**The spirit's voice depends on the spirit — and the spirit depends on who you really are.** It runs in parallel to personality. A calm person tends to bond something calm: it feels like a gentle breeze at the back of the neck and speaks like a teacher who's never once raised their voice. A fiery person runs hot-blooded: the spirit feels like an engine idling at the edge of a roar, and it speaks with *passion* — not volume, passion, every word weighted like it matters. But the match isn't always the obvious one. Sometimes a quiet, mild person bonds something hot-blooded and impatient — because underneath the calm, that's what they actually are, and the spirit knew before they did. The bond can tell you something true about yourself that you hadn't admitted yet. *(These are tendencies, not rules — name what fits your character.)*

**Also establishes:** the MHA-quirk baseline. *Everyone* has a spirit; the vast majority are mundane — a small domestic thing, a faint useful knack, nothing that ever sees a fight. Only a few have the training, the desire, or the raw Spark capacity to push it into the hero/villain/anti-hero/antagonist range. This is why Sora is a functioning city and not a war zone: the bonded population is universal, the *combatant* population is tiny.

*[Filter check: the MHA baseline is a quietly huge worldbuilding fix — it makes the city plausible and gives every civilian beat a grounded reason to exist. ### 1.6 — Spark geography, the imbalance & the tech lineage ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: Sora's residents developed will-magic and became its greatest practitioners. They shared knowledge, but the strongest Sparks tend to be BORN in Sora — descended from the will-workers. Other places can get a strong Spark but not everywhere, not reliably. This creates the imbalance, the need for the wider world, and the eventual development of artificial ability via tech. Tone: Boruto-level tech (woven through daily life, works), two-lineages-one-descent, with a touch of reclaimed. World = anime fantasy-tech (Made in Abyss-ish but a bit more advanced; not modern, not dark ages).

**Fleshed:**

**Spark is in the blood of Sora, and the blood remembers.** Everyone everywhere is born with a Spark — that's universal. But the *strength* of the inclination isn't spread evenly across the world. It runs thickest in Sora, because Sora is where the first practitioners learned to work the world by will, and the long erosion of generations carried that talent down through Soran blood like a deep river. The strongest combatant-tier Sparks are, overwhelmingly, *born here*. Soran parents, Soran ground, Soran descent. Knowledge was shared outward over the centuries — other places learned the forms — but you can teach the technique and still not be born with the river. Elsewhere, a powerful Spark surfaces rarely and unpredictably, a lightning-strike instead of a bloodline.

**That imbalance is the engine of the wider world.** Every settlement beyond Sora needs Sparks they cannot reliably grow, for the same reasons any society needs its protectors, its delvers, its powered hands — and they cannot simply *make more*. Sora has a natural monopoly on the one resource that can't be exported in a crate. This is the gravity that pulls the world toward Sora and pushes the rest of the world to find another answer.

**And the answer the world built is tech.** If you cannot be born with a combatant-tier Spark, you *engineer* the capability. This is the second lineage — and it shares one root with the first. Will-magic descended two ways: into the **Spark** for those born to hold it, and into **craft** for those who weren't. The augmented, the forged, the artificed — their power is humanity's clever, stubborn workaround for being locked out of the natural inheritance. It's not a rival magic from nowhere; it's the same impulse ("bend the world to intent") routed through tools instead of blood. That makes the Augmented a *poignant* choice in play, not a sci-fi bolt-on: it's the path of someone who refused to accept that the river skipped them.

**Tech-level: lived-in mid-tech.** Boruto-grade — woven through daily life and it *works*: transit, lighting, comms, the infrastructure of a real functioning city. Past medieval, short of modern. The world reads as anime fantasy-tech: traditional power (the bond) and everyday tech sitting side by side without anyone finding it strange. **A touch of reclaimed underneath:** the *best* tech — the stuff nobody can fully replicate — is reverse-engineered from relics hauled out of the deep, the wreckage of the first Sora that outclasses modern craft. So modern tech is the everyday floor; relic-tech is the unreachable ceiling, glimpsed and misunderstood. Climbing back up toward something the past already had.

*[Filter check: this is the keystone that makes the whole world rhyme — Spark-geography explains Sora's centrality, the imbalance explains why the wider world exists and matters, the tech-as-craft-cousin removes the seam (tech and bond share one descent, not two unrelated systems), and the reclaimed-relic thread ties straight back to Appendix M's founding myth. Augmented reframed from bolt-on to character. References (Boruto/Made in Abyss) are calibration only — never appear in-game.]*

**↳ CANON SET:** strongest Sparks born in Sora (bloodline-thick descent from will-workers); other settlements get strong Sparks rarely/unreliably; this imbalance drives the wider world's need + the tech/augmentation lineage; tech = craft-branch of the same will-magic descent; Boruto-level everyday tech + reclaimed relic-tech from the deep as the unreachable wonder.

## PILLAR 2 — THE CITY OF SORA *(building now)*

Framework + blanks. We already have hard canon: 10 named districts, a bottomless central dungeon, the MHA-baseline (everyone has a spirit, most mundane). This pillar breathes life into what already has a name. Same method — Kyle throws, Claude shapes.

### 2.1 — What IS Sora? The shape of the place ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: A city-state. There are other locations, other dungeons — Sora is one of the most, if not THE most, famous. Many legends were made and born here. Built around the dungeon (the first Sora, now calcified below). Sora feels like life — lived-in, imperfect, NOT the utopia the founder wished. A place like no other where one line has persevered. "Sora is the sky, always looking up" — distorted from the original founding phrase but the same emotional resonance.

**Fleshed:**

Sora is a city-state — one power among many, not the whole world. There are other cities, other settlements, and there are **other dungeons** scattered across the world, each with its own delvers and dangers. But Sora is the famous one. Maybe the most famous there is. The legends people tell in far towns were *made here* — born in the deep beneath these streets and carried outward until they belonged to everyone. People come to Sora the way they go to a place that matters.

And it is not a utopia. Whatever the first dreamers wanted this to be, what actually grew is a real city: lived-in, uneven, imperfect. There are good streets and bad ones, fortunes made and lives ground down, beauty and rot pressed up against each other. What makes Sora extraordinary isn't perfection — it's *persistence*. One line, one thread, has held here across a span of time nobody can really fathom, and the city is the proof of it. A place like no other not because it's clean, but because it *endured*.

The thing every Soran feels without quite saying: **Sora is the sky.** The whole city, built up out of a hole in the ground, is always somehow looking *up* — reaching, climbing, refusing to be defined by the dark it came from. People say it as a half-remembered phrase, worn smooth, not knowing it's the descendant of something a founder said when there was nothing here but deep. The words have drifted. The feeling hasn't moved an inch.

**And the words have drifted further than that.** What began as an aspiration — *we reach for what's above us* — has, over two millennia of being the famous city, the powerful city, the city everyone needs, quietly curdled into something else. The phrase Sorans say now isn't quite the same phrase their founders meant. The modern version doesn't reach upward; it looks around and sees nothing worth looking up to. **"We are the sky."** Subtle, almost polite — but the implication is there: the pinnacle. What else would you look up at? *(Note-for-us: the original meant aspiration; the current means arrival. Same words, opposite direction. Player-facing it's just a phrase people say — only a very careful player will notice the inversion.)*

*[Filter check: city-state-among-many opens the world without diluting Sora's centrality (it's the FAMOUS one — ties to the Spark-geography monopoly in 1.6). "Not a utopia" is the honest, lived-in texture that keeps it FONT and not the warm bible. "Sora is the sky" = the founding-phrase descended trace from Appendix M, surfacing as folk-saying. "We are the sky" = the modern drift — civic pride tipped into quiet hubris, same words opposite direction, player-facing subtext only. Other dungeons established as canon — useful later.]*

### 2.2 — Daily life & the Guild ✨
> KYLE'S IDEA: We know daily life now — spirits (natural to artificial) used in everything; the driving force of the world. Being a combatant happens for the same reason crime happens. Managed by a wider GUILD NETWORK that takes jobs and classifies members. Some choose not to join. Crimes are also posted to the Guild to be solved. (Guild expanded later.) The Bleed already covered.

**Fleshed:**

Power is simply *infrastructure* in Sora. Spirits run through everything — the mundane ones doing the quiet work of a functioning city (the heat that bakes the bread, the hands that move faster than hands should, the small useful knacks nobody thinks twice about), and the engineered/augmented abilities filling the gaps for those born without a strong Spark. Between the born and the built, ability is the engine the whole world turns on. It isn't special. It's *Tuesday*.

**Combatants exist for the same reason crime does** — because where there's power, there's the use and misuse of it, and someone always reaches for more. That whole tangled economy is managed (loosely) by the **Guild**: a wider network that takes jobs, posts contracts, and *classifies* its members by capability. Need a dungeon cleared, a threat handled, a problem solved? It goes to the Guild as a job. Committed a crime, or got one committed against you? That gets posted to the Guild too — as a contract for someone to solve. The Guild is the clearinghouse between people with power and people who need it pointed at something.

Crucially: **some people don't join.** The Guild classifies and contracts, but it doesn't *own* you. Plenty of powered people operate outside it entirely — by choice, by necessity, or because the Guild posted a contract with their name on it. This is why the Guild can't define your character as hero or villain: it's a jobs-and-classification network, not a moral authority. You decide what you are. The Guild just decides what rank to file you under. *(Full Guild structure — the Amatsunagi Consortium, the Celestial Assembly, ranks and politics — fleshed in the dedicated Guild section below.)*

#### RANK (D→S) — earned through faction progression
Rank is your **public certified delving depth** — how deep the city licenses you to go, and how it treats you. It is **separate from Spark** (Spark is your private interior power; Rank is your external standing — the two can mismatch, and that gap is story: a high-Spark unknown with no rank, or a low-Spark delver certified past their depth by a lucky feat).

**Rank is earned by advancing a faction, not by grinding floors.** Each faction is a path into Mugen with its own way of certifying you. As you climb a faction's standing track (Favor/Conviction), your rank rises in bands — every 2 standing = one band:

| Standing | Rank |
|---|---|
| 2 (join) | **D** — the floor |
| 4 | **C** |
| 6 | **B** |
| 8 | **A** |
| 10 (final event) | **S** — ceiling of certified delving |

*(No E rank — D is the floor. Below S lies the Uncharted, which no rank covers: the central mystery.)*

This makes the faction track *the* rank ladder — advancing any faction certifies you upward. **Checkpoint principle (from the original design):** rank is *permission, not a wall.* A feat can certify you straight to a band without re-treading the ones below, and going deeper than your rank isn't forbidden — it's just how people die. The freedom to descend past your certification is a real choice with teeth.

**The band is universal (D→S); the TITLE is per-faction.** Same rank, different word depending on who certified you. A B-band Guild contractor and a B-band Cultist are equals in depth but wear different names. Faction titles by band:
- **The Guild:** **D / C / B / A — "[Rank] Binder"** (the plain certification — a C-Rank Binder is a C-Rank Binder, no flourish). At **S — the binder earns an epithet:** *S-Rank Binder: [name the city gives them]* (e.g. "S-Rank Binder: Gale the Swift"). D through A is a file entry; S is a name people say. The epithet is the prestige marker — the rank certifies, the name distinguishes. *(Epithet pool to be written later — "The Morning Sun," "The Sharp" as tone examples, not canon yet.)*
- **The Cult of Mugen** (monastic, descent-as-holiness): **D — Penitent · C — Limit-Seeker · B — Deepwalker · A — Abyssal · S — Boundless.** Rank measured by how deep you've survived and how close to the bottomless you'll push.
- **The Veneration of the Makami Shu** (sainthood, virtue-as-standing — the *Impression* ladder): **D — Echo · C — Reflection · B — Mirror · A — Vessel · S — Avatar.** You climb from faintly echoing a Saint's impression to becoming a vessel of one — sharing vocabulary with the Avatar Catch (the faith's glory-ceiling). *(Note: "Architect" is reserved — Kure's own title, never a common rank, to protect his rarity.)*
*(Five factions share the D→S band system; each carries its own title ladder. Two are built — the Guild and Ryuugenism. Three remain to be designed.)*

#### THE GUILD — the Amatsunagi Consortium ✨

> KYLE'S LORE (worked & refined below): formal name Amatsunagi Consortium, known to everyone as "the Guild." Most prominent faction in Sora. Descended from the founding council. Run by the Celestial Assembly — S-rank binders, voted every 5 years, governing by proposal. Merit-absolute, no fast-tracks. Wants Sora's prosperity, funds itself off the dungeon and a cut of contracts. Seen as a necessary frustration. Uneasy-but-positive coexistence with Ryuugenism, soured by the faith's binders refusing to register.

**The name.** Formally the **Amatsunagi Consortium**. Nobody calls it that unless they're being cold, official, or making a point — on the street it's just **the Guild**. The gap between the two names is a tone lever: a clerk who says "the Amatsunagi Consortium" to your face is reminding you where you stand.

**What it descends from.** The Guild is the eroded descendant of the founding council that once governed Sora. Across the centuries its function and facilities drifted — from *running a city built on co-existence* to *licensing, classifying, and taxing powered labour.* It does not remember governing anything nobler; the institution simply became what the present needed and forgot it was ever otherwise. *(↳ Founding trace: an institution whose origin it has lost. See Appendix M, Trace ⑤ — the same forgetting that hollowed the faith hollowed the council into a clearinghouse.)*

**Who runs it — the Celestial Assembly.** The Guild's governing body, made up of **S-rank binders who chose to point themselves at Sora-as-a-whole** rather than keep climbing or cashing out. Some join by conviction — the ceiling reached, they turn to service. Some are there because injury took the choice from them: an S-rank who can't delve anymore but still serves. Both carry weight; the wounded administrator who governs the deep they can no longer enter is a particular kind of figure in Sora. The Assembly is **voted in every five years** and oversees the Guild's day-to-day operations.

**How it governs — proposal, not decree.** The Assembly functions as Sora's **loose government**, but it cannot rule by fiat. It can *suggest* and *propose* — and any proposal of consequence needs the support of a **majority of the five factions (3 of 5)** to pass. The Guild is one of the five. This makes big decisions genuinely hard to land, and that difficulty is the point: Sora's freedom is partly protected by how slow its government is to agree. The five-faction vote is permanent low-grade political tension, and the faction clash (Pillar 2.6) runs straight through it.

**The creed — merit, absolutely.** The Guild's defining virtue and its defining frustration. **Regardless of family, wealth, or status, you are evaluated and you climb like everyone else. No fast-tracks, no bought ranks, no exceptions.** Critics say the Guild is too rule-abiding, too rigid, too in love with its own procedures — and they're not wrong. But the rigidity is *load-bearing*: it's why a nobody from the Ashfields and an outer-ring heir hold the same C-Rank Binder card and mean the same thing by it. The Guild would rather be frustrating than corrupt.

**What it wants.** **Sora's prosperity** — sincerely, if self-servingly. The dungeon is the engine: through Mugen the Guild generates profit, and through the contracts it posts and brokers, powered individuals pay it a cut. Its interests and the city's are *mostly* aligned, which is exactly why it's tolerated. The Guild is seen as a **necessary frustration** — the people of Sora enjoy their freedom and quietly understand that something has to monitor powered individuals to some degree. Nobody loves the Guild. Most are glad it exists. Everybody resents it on a Tuesday.

**Binders.** Powered people are **binders**, full stop — the universal word, Guild-coined and city-adopted. Rank attaches directly: *D-Rank Binder, C-Rank Binder*, up the ladder. The title is deliberately plain; the Guild doesn't dress up certification. (The exception is S — see the title ladder above — where the binder earns an epithet and becomes a name the city says.)

**The Ryuugenism friction.** The Guild and the faith hold a **positive but uneasy coexistence.** The Guild accepts Ryuugenism's beliefs and broadly follows its principles — this is a devout city and the Guild is part of it. But for **decades** there has been one grinding point of contention: **many of the faith's binders refuse to register in the Guild's archives.** The faith holds that matters of the city and matters of religion should stay separate — a binder's depth before the Saints or before Mugen is nobody's bureaucratic business. ## PILLAR 3 — THE DUNGEON ✨

### 3.1 — What Mugen actually is

Nobody calls it a city. Nobody has called it a city in living memory, or in the memory before that, or in the memory before that. It is the dungeon. It has always been the dungeon. The entrance is Mugen — infinite — and below Mugen is the deep, and below the deep is the Uncharted, and that is the whole of what Sora knows about the thing beneath its feet.

What Sora does not know: it is standing on top of its own origin.

The dungeon is old Sora. The founding settlement — built downward, stratum by stratum, a city constructed in the deep before anyone thought to reach for the surface. When the people finally broke through to open sky they kept going, the way people do, and the underground city was not abandoned all at once but gradually, the way homes are always abandoned — a district emptied here, a passage sealed there, a collapse that nobody repaired because there was more room above. Over generations the memory of what it had been eroded. The city above grew and forgot the city below. The deep became the dungeon. The dungeon became the thing that feeds Sora and kills its delvers, beloved adversary, the constant that every Soran has always lived with.

The founding structures are still down there. Deep enough that no present-day delver has reached them intact. The grand library in disrepair, still holding texts in a script nobody living reads correctly. Parts of the original workshop. Chambers built for purposes that present-day Sora would not recognise and could not name. The further down you go, the more the stone starts to look *made* rather than natural — shaped with an intention that the current age cannot replicate and mostly does not notice.

None of this is known. The player who reaches those depths feels something old and does not have the language for what they are standing in. That is correct. That is the only honest way to encounter it.

---

### 3.2 — What the descent feels like

The dungeon does not hate you. That is the first thing a delver learns, usually without being able to say it plainly. There is warmth in the deep — not temperature, not comfort, something older than both. The Sorans who have spent their lives descending call it the pull. The Cult calls it the mouth of a living god. The Venerators call it the weight of the Saints' presence. The Guild classifies it as ambient Spark-density above baseline and moves on.

What it actually is: two thousand years of genuine intention soaked into the stone. The Will accumulated where the founding city built correctly, and the building lasted, and the accumulation never left when the people did. You descend into something that was made with care by people who are gone, and the care is still there, and it does not know you but it does not turn you away either.

**The dungeon's ask is contribution.** Not sacrifice — the deep does not want you dead, it is not hungry for that. What it asks, in the way that a place can ask without speaking, is what you are willing to put in. How deep are you willing to go. What are you willing to carry down and leave there. Every delver answers this differently. Most answer it at the first threshold and come back up. A few keep going. Fewer still reach the place where the question stops feeling like a question and starts feeling like a weight you carry in the chest, low and constant, and you understand that the deep has been patient for a very long time.

**The descent shifts in stages**, and the thresholds are felt before they are understood:

**Upper strata** — the warmth is present but faint. Familiar. This is where Sora has always sent its D and C rank delvers, where the danger is real but calibrated to people who are still learning what they are. The spirits here are loose, hostile, unbound — a pulling pressure in the core, the body's warning that something powered is nearby and not on your side. The ground underfoot feels worked. Most delvers who notice this assume it is just how deep stone looks.

**Middle strata** — the warmth deepens and so does the dark. The spirits are stronger here, more present, beginning to take on weight and form that the upper strata's creatures don't have. The passages start to feel like they were built for foot traffic. The stone has a different quality — not natural formation, shaped. B and A rank delvers operate here. The thresholds cost something real to cross and the cost is different for everyone. What you find here — ores, herbs, fragments of worked material — cannot be found above. The Guild bulletin posts requests for exactly these things. The people who fill those requests live in the inner ring and know what the crossing costs and go anyway.

**Deep strata — the edge of old Sora** — where S rank certification runs out and the Uncharted begins. The warmth here has a second note underneath it, low and unresolved, something that has been sitting in the stone for long enough that it has its own gravity. The inverse weight is here too — two millennia of abandonment, dark things moving into empty halls, the accumulated texture of a place that was left and did not stay empty. Both are real. Neither cancels the other. The bittersweet is not a metaphor: it is the specific sensation of being in a lost home that does not recognise you but still feels, somehow, like yours.

The spirits at this depth are different in kind, not just degree. Not loose and hostile — *whole*. Physical incarnations, fully present, the kind of thing that takes up space in a room the way a person does. Beasts that have been down here long enough to become something more than beasts. And shades — shapes that move like people, carry the posture and weight of someone who once lived in these halls. The dungeon does not mean anything by them. They are simply what accumulates in a place that was lived in and then left. They are not the founders. They do not know anything. But they move like someone who did, and that is its own kind of wrong.

---

### 3.3 — What the deep produces

The Guild's bulletin board handles civilian requests — herbs, ores, compounds, materials that exist nowhere else and that the surface city consumes without knowing where they come from. A doctor needs a cultivar that only grows in the middle strata's ambient conditions. A builder needs an ore that only forms under deep pressure. Fae is waiting on a material that has not come up yet. These requests sit on the board and delvers fill them for standing, coin, or both. The supply chain of Sora runs through Mugen whether Sora thinks about it or not.

Beyond the material, the deep offers what no surface faction can: **spirits worth binding.**

The further down you go, the rarer and more powerful the unbound spirits become. A delver who bonds a spirit from the upper strata has something useful. A delver who reaches the middle strata and survives the binding has something the city notices. The spirit grades by depth:

**Common** — upper strata, loose and hostile, the standard risk of an early delve. Useful. Plentiful. The majority of bonded spirits in Sora come from here.

**Rare** — middle strata. More wholly formed, harder to approach, harder to bind. The bonding costs something real — the spirit has enough presence to push back, and what you bring to the negotiation matters. A rare spirit changes a binder's standing in the city.

**Mythic** — deep strata, near the edge of the Uncharted. Few delvers reach these. Fewer survive the encounter, let alone the binding. A mythic spirit is fully incarnate — weight, presence, force. It is not looking for a binder. Whether it accepts one is a question the delver answers with everything they have.

**Legendary** — the Uncharted. Theoretical for most. The accounts that come back from delvers who have glimpsed this depth describe something that does not feel like an encounter with a spirit. It feels like stumbling onto something enormous. The words for it don't come easily. Nobody has confirmed a legendary binding. Nobody has confirmed what's down there at all.

The cost scales with the grade. A deeper spirit demands more of the person attempting the bond — more Spark, more clarity of intent, more willingness to be changed by what answers. The bond is always a relationship. Down here, the other party in that relationship has been alone in the dark for a very long time, and what it has become in the waiting is not something a shallow reading of the Spark can handle.

---

### 3.4 — The Uncharted

Below the deepest S-rank certified depth. Below where the oldest confirmed delvers have returned from. The place the factions all point toward and none have claimed.

What it feels like, from the accounts of those who have brushed its edge and come back: something enormous. Not hostile. Not welcoming. Just *present*, in the way that very old things are present — a weight that has nothing to prove and no interest in your reaction to it. The delvers who have felt it describe the sensation differently each time, which is either proof that it is different for everyone or proof that language fails at that depth. Both may be true.

What is down there: the deepest founding structures. The original anchor. The densest accumulation of Will in either direction — the founding intention and the long inverse weight of abandonment, both at their maximum, occupying the same stone. A present-day delver who reached it would have no framework for what they were standing in. They would feel it anyway. The feeling would not leave them.

Nobody has made it. The Uncharted is not a mystery to be solved in the app. It is the gravity that makes everything above it matter — the question that the factions orbit, the thing that the Cult descends toward as prayer, the thing that the deepest delvers move toward because the pull does not stop asking. It is what lies below a city that has spent two thousand years looking up.

*[Filter check: the founding history stays buried — no player text names old Sora, Kure, the Will, or the anchor. What surfaces is sensation: the warmth, the bittersweet, the weight of something made with care by people who are gone. The spirit grade system (Common→Rare→Mythic→Legendary) maps cleanly onto the rank bands without requiring a parallel mechanical system. The inverse weight is present as texture, never named in-app. The Uncharted is defined by sensation only — no reveal, no confirmation, no floor. ## PILLAR 4 — POWERS THAT BE / FACTIONS *(building now)*

### 4.1 — RYUUGENISM *(the first faction-set — fleshed)*

**What it is, structurally:** Ryuugenism is BOTH a cross-cutting faith AND a faction-set. As a *religion* it cuts across the city — a Guild contractor, an outer-ring merchant, and an inner-ring delver can all be believers without belonging to any temple. As a *faction* its organised arms are the two rival sects below, which post contracts, hold standing, and clash. So: the faith is the belief most Sorans loosely hold or shrug at; the two sects are the institutional bodies a player can actually align with.

**How known it is:** the faith is **mainstream, not fringe** — most Sorans believe in the Saints and hold some form of Ryuugenism the way real cities hold a folk-religion. What's *unverified* isn't whether people are devout; it's the **original history, which survives only in fragments and has been steadily misinterpreted.** The Saints were *real founders* — but that truth is forgotten. Across two thousand years they've been inflated from people who built a city into larger-than-life saints and near-deities. The faithful sincerely venerate them; they simply no longer know they're venerating *founders* rather than divine beings. **This is the descended-myth rule (Appendix M) working as designed:** the faith is the main channel the founding myth survived through — widely believed, deeply distorted, the accurate version lost to almost everyone. The player, through the deep's fragments, can glimpse the history the entire devout city has forgotten.

**The root and the schism:** Named for **Ryuugen (the Deep Origin)** — in modern scripture no longer remembered as anything but "the Patience that Preceded All Things." The core belief: the Spark in your blood is a divine inheritance meant to bend the universe to human intent.

**The faith is ancient** — its oldest notations imply it has existed since Sora's founding. Scripture scholars interpret the city itself as holy ground, protected by the Saints. For most of its existence it was one unified faith. Then, approximately **400 years ago**, a schism split it in two — the founding of the two rival sects that persist to the present day.

**Lady Sila and Prophet Leona — the founders of the schism (remembered accurately; 400 years is recent):**
Two high priestesses of the faith disagreed on its core teaching.
- **Lady Sila** — a fierce warrior whose Spark leaned toward raw combat, manifesting as her spirit **Everbreak** (the spirit of the limitless push, of reaching past every ceiling). She was argued by contemporaries to be as close to Okami as any person had come — *though see the Okami/Kure drift note below for what the faith got wrong about this.* Her grievance: the faithful had grown weak and content through plenty; without struggle there's no improvement. Her answer: **push the limitless boundary, and the offering to Mugen is the challenge itself.** She broke away to found the Cult.
- **Prophet Leona** — her Spark leaned toward the concept of the future, manifesting as the rare spirit **Foreseen** (a spirit of divination and precognition, genuinely unusual). She read her gift as proof: a life following the Saints' virtues had earned her something no one could train for. Her teaching: **follow the virtues of those so great they became Saints; the path is sustained peace.** She held the line of the original faith, which became the Veneration.

**The fault line between them:** Sila saw Leona's way as *stagnant* — reverence without striving, worshipping a ceiling instead of climbing toward it. Leona saw Sila's way as *reckless* — embodying one Saint's aspect and refusing the balance all five demanded, progress as hubris. Neither was entirely wrong about the other. That argument is now a 400-year-old institutional rivalry.

**The Okami/Kure drift (NOTE-FOR-US — walled off from player):** The Cult reveres Okami most, believing she represents the drive to push past limits. This is a theological error that calcified over time. Okami's true domain is *protection and combat — fighting so everyone can protect themselves.* The drive to push past limits, to strive endlessly upward, is actually **Kure's** attribute. The Cult latched onto Okami because "First Blade" reads as combat, and combat reads as striving and power. The secondary mechanism: Kure's blessing is the *rarest*, and tends toward builder-leaning Sparks — which Cultists, skewing toward physical combat, almost never have. So in practice Kure feels distant and abstract to them; Okami feels immediate and proven. The misread is self-reinforcing. *(Player-facing: the Cult sincerely believes in Okami as their patron. A very careful player might notice the inconsistency. The Cult never will.)*

**THE BURIED IRONY (the keystone — a whole city worships a memory it has misread):** the Saints were founders; the faith made them gods. And underneath even that, both sects pray to the same root — Will-Magic, the Spark in the blood. The Cult's "Blessings" and the Venerators' "Virtues" are just modern distorted names for the *same power both sides already carry* — the name-the-intent power the founders used to build the city. Neither side realises they're worshipping the thing in their own chest, raised to a god and pointed back at them. The player can come to understand this through the deep's fragments; the devout city never does. *(Mechanical gift: a delver who grasps the irony might bond differently than one who believes the dogma.)* **This stays in our docs — not in the app. Too much information for a player-facing panel.**

**The House of Sora — the shared temple:** the faith's physical home in the city. The Cult and Veneration are rivals who nonetheless share the same institution — occupying **different sections of the same temple**, yet sharing the scripture library and the sacred item vault. A strained coexistence built into the architecture. The name carries the quiet Soran hubris: the **House of Sora** — the city's spiritual heart, which also implies the house that *is* Sora, the best of what the city believes about itself.

**The Library of the 5:** one of Ryuugenism's greatest treasures, held within the House of Sora. Scholars and citizens alike receive sermons here; scholars debate and study the scripture. For those highest in the church, access to the **legendary holy items** — locked away and considered sacred. *(Note-for-us: the library is impressive-looking but historically useless — 2,000 years of misinterpretation has corrupted the texts. The holy items are revered founding-era artefacts that nobody can read correctly anymore. The medicine and scholarly marvels the faithful point to as proof of the Saints' greatness are real but pale against what founding-era Sora actually produced — they don't know they're living in a diminished age. Player-facing: just a grand institution with a sacred vault.)*

#### THE CULT OF MUGEN — "the Strata-Strand" (deep-worship, Okami-rooted)
To the Cult, **Mugen is the mouth of a living god** and the offering is the challenge itself — descending is prayer. They trace their origin to **Lady Sila**, whose spirit Everbreak manifested the limitless push; her closeness to Okami (as they believe it) validated the path. They revere Okami most of any Saint — the First Blade, patron of combat and protection, whom they interpret as the Saint of striving past limits. *(The Okami/Kure drift is walled off — see above. Player-facing: the Cult's doctrine is sincere and internally consistent.)*
- **Manifestation (physical blessings):** raw bodily enhancement, rarely elegant — laws like Density, Combustion, Momentum.
- **Sensory signature:** power feels like *an engine idling at the edge of a roar.* Active, it's grinding stone and heavy breathing — the oddly-warming pressure of the strata pressing back. *(FONT register: stone, body, weight, pressure — correct.)*
- **The Tempering Loop (mechanic):** belief feeds power. The more a Cultist trusts they're improving against the dungeon's hostility, the stronger their physical blessing — conviction in the Infinite Below fuels the body's infinite potential.
- **The Futility Break (mechanic):** if a Cultist loses faith — decides the dungeon is a truly endless, worthless void — their intent collapses into a **Negative Break**. Left frightened in a way that doesn't fully heal, their back suddenly bare in the dark.

#### THE VENERATION OF THE MAKAMI SHU — "the Spirit-Strand" (founder-worship, virtue-rooted)
Where the Cult looks at the stone, the Venerators look at the **Saints** — the founders warped into a celestial hierarchy of architects who commanded *True-Naming* to shape the world. Their origin is **Prophet Leona**, whose rare spirit **Foreseen** (divination and precognition) she read as proof: following the Saints' virtues earned her something no training could produce. Her doctrine: revere those so great they became Saints; the path is sustained peace; we can only hope to come close to them. Practitioners seek to mirror the strong impressions the founders left.
- **The Five (mirror their legendary traits):**
  - **Okami (the First Blade)** — Law and Order. Followers manifest a *grounded, immovable steadiness* — the discipline of holding a line that does not break.
  - **Makami (the Swift)** — Intent. Speed is nothing without a fully *decided* aim; followers manifest committed, single-purpose motion.
  - **Roya (Saintess of the Web)** — Precision. Devotees manifest the *exactness of a held breath before the word* — knowing the pattern, seeing the betrayal before the knife.
  - **Okuri (the Stalwart Sage)** — the Record. "The right moment always arrives." Heavy, patient, stone-still presences that endure and remember.
  - **Kure (Master of Foundations)** — the Architect. Rarely invoked; his blessing is said to be a *fundamental shift* in reality itself.
- **Manifestation (spiritual virtues):** their spirits act like *teachers who never raise their voices*, mirroring a Saint's trait. They obsess over **relics** — fragments and inscriptions hauled from the deep in a script no one living can read — misreading common tools and dungeon-debris as divine weapons. *(This is Appendix M's "Artifacts carry a myth-fragment layer" surfacing as faction behaviour.)*
- **Sensory register (FONT, not electric):** the weight of a held breath, the cool of old worked stone, the exactness of a blade's edge, the stillness before a name is spoken. *(Corrected from the imported circuitry/ozone palette — Venerators are naming/precision/record/patience, expressed in stone, breath, blade, salt.)*

#### THE FAITH-BOND & THE AVATAR CATCH (shared)
In Ryuugenism the bond is a direct reflection of **conviction** — faith is the forge that hammers the Spark into a defined shape. Because power is rooted in belief, a crisis of faith is a physical catastrophe: to doubt the Saint, or the value of the Tempering, is to risk a Bond Break. **The Avatar (the Catch):** in peak fervour — or in the very instant of a break — a practitioner can reach back across the break and hold. The Refusal: screaming *no, not like this* and meaning it hard enough to make the world answer, becoming for that instant an Avatar of the Saint or the Deep. *(This is the glory-ceiling break-catch from Pillar 1.3, expressed in the faith's own terms.)*

#### CONVICTION (the faction standing track — TWO tracks, one per sect)
*Standing in Ryuugenism is called **Conviction** — the spiritual gravity you exert within the faith. It is tracked **per sect**: a **Cult Conviction** and a **Veneration Conviction**, each rising through that sect's own missions, delving, and rival-clashes (the standing loop from Pillar 2). You can hold standing in both, but they pull against each other — cousins who agree on nothing.*

- **6 — B-band.** A name people in the faith know.
- **7 — Relic of the First.** A misread founding-fragment comes to you. *Define what it is, what it does no modern spirit explains, and why the scholars have its purpose wrong.* (Appendix M's myth-fragment layer.)

Drift your devotion toward the other sect over a campaign and your path can shift — the faith is about conviction, and conviction can change.*

### 4.2 — SORAN CULTURE · THE MERCY DOCTRINE ✨

**Origin — Makami and the zero-casualty war.** In the founding era, Makami — one of the figures who would become a Saint — held a philosophy of zero casualties in war. Not mercy as softness: mercy as strategy, as confidence, as the long view. The reasoning was precise: the capable enemy you spare today is the capable ally you might need tomorrow. Finish someone who's down and you've spent a resource you didn't have to.

Over 1,600 years this philosophy descended from tactical doctrine into cultural texture. Present-day Sora doesn't think of it as a rule or a law. It's simply *how things are done* — the way a city carries an inherited instinct without knowing where it came from.

**The saying.** The distilled form that survived:

> *"Only the afraid finish what they started."*

The implications are layered the way Soran arrogance usually is: finishing someone who's down is *fear* — fear they'll recover, fear you'll need to face them again, fear that mercy is weakness. The Soran read is the inverse: if you're strong enough, you can afford to leave them standing. Mercy is the flex.

**What it looks like in practice.** A defeated opponent in Sora is rarely killed outright. They're humiliated, contained, driven off, made to owe something. The city's conflict culture runs on *standing*, not elimination — you don't erase a rival, you *outrank* them. This is partly the Guild's influence (assassination is bad for business; a living debtor is more useful than a dead one) and partly the inherited doctrine running so deep nobody questions it.

**The arrogance underneath.** Sora as a city genuinely believes it is the strongest power in the known world. That belief makes mercy *easy* — you can afford not to finish someone when you're confident they can never actually threaten you. It's a kindness with an edge of condescension built into it, and most Sorans don't notice. The few who do find it insufferable. The rest find it obvious.

**Where it breaks.** The doctrine has limits the city prefers not to examine. A threat that *genuinely* can't be contained — something from the deep that doesn't respond to mercy logic, or a rival power that takes Soran restraint as an invitation — is the city's blind spot. Sora's confidence in its own dominance is what makes the mercy doctrine work. The day that confidence is wrong, the doctrine becomes a liability. *(Note-for-us: this is not a present-day plot thread — it's a cultural texture and a tonal undercurrent. The player feels it. Nobody in-world is stating it.)*

*[Filter check: descended from real founding lore (Makami's philosophy) without surfacing Makami as a character — it's the idea that survived, not the person. "Only the afraid finish what they started" earns its place as a line the city actually says. The arrogance-as-confidence framing keeps Sora from reading as naively pacifist — it's a powerful city that acts like one. The break-condition is noted for texture without becoming a plot hook we're committed to.]*

**The myth in one breath (the legend, already distorted):** Long ago, people built a city *downward* — into the deep, stratum by stratum, reaching not down but *up*, because something built at the bottom should always reach for the sky. The city grew up and over its own foundations. The deep strata it was born in calcified, emptied, became the **bottomless dungeon** beneath present-day Sora. The founders blurred into fable. ### HOW THE PAST SURFACES — and how it gets scuffed
*Kyle: the first Sora isn't gone — it's intermittently excavated and usually misread, exactly like real history. A delver hauls up a fragment; a scholar links it tenuously to the legend; a popular version mangles it; the truth stays out of reach. Most people don't care to get it right, so every handling drifts the interpretation further.*

The deep still holds the first Sora — not as a lost city you can visit, but as **fragments**. A delver cracks a sealed chamber and finds an inscription in a construction-tongue nobody speaks. A worn relic comes up that does something no modern spirit explains. A shape in the stone that looks *made*. These surface a few times a generation, and what happens next is the texture:

- **The careful scholar** (rare) gets *part* of it right and over-reaches on the rest — links a fragment tenuously to the founding legend, publishes, is half-correct in ways they can't tell from the half they invented. The most reliable source, and still wrong.
- **The delver** (common) doesn't care what it *means* — they care what it *sells for*. A genuine founding-relic ends up in a Saltmarket stall mislabelled, or melted for its materials, the inscription lost because nobody read it.
- **The street version** (everywhere) is pure distortion — the bones of a true thing wrapped in whatever makes a better story. "They say the dungeon was dug by a god." "They say the first ones could speak things into being." (That last one is *true* — will-magic — but it's told as a fairy tale and believed by no one serious.)

**The truth is always recoverable in fragments and never in full.** You get a glimpse, never the story. This is the honest historical texture: a real past, genuinely down there, perpetually misread by a present that mostly can't be bothered.

### 4.3 — THE WELD *(fleshed)*

**What it is:** The only place in Sora that makes spirit-infused items. Not a shop — a workshop that takes commissions. The foci they produce let binders channel their Spark's properties in focused, specific, and often entirely unique ways: a fire-Spark compressed into a cutting edge rather than general combustion; an impact-Spark stored and released in a single moment rather than dissipated; a sound-Spark directed through a specific medium rather than broadcast. The item doesn't give you power. It shapes how your power expresses.

**Origin (fragment principle — the Weld doesn't know this cleanly):** Somewhere in the lineage are three figures from the founding era: Oniroku — the builder who waited 43 years to receive a vision and built 412 structures from it, whose inscription above the original craft house read *"What is made with genuine intention carries that intention forward"* — Gorr, the construction lead whose only credential question was *"Can you lift stone"* — and the Amatsukōbō, the original spirit-item workshop founded by the city's first maker, which produced items in two categories: citizen items (made with joy) and weapons (made with regret). Two thousand years of drift. The philosophy survived; the history didn't. The Weld practices a tradition it can't fully name from sources it can no longer trace.

**Location:** Ashfields, inner ring. The industrial quarter.

**Neutrality:** Absolute. The Weld makes for anyone — Guild contractors, Titan fighters, faith devotees, unregistered binders. The only gate is standing, not allegiance.

**The mechanic that makes them unique:** A Builder's spirit bleeds into the work without exception. They cannot mask their emotional state during crafting — it enters the item. A commission completed under resentment, distraction, or exhaustion produces something technically functional and spiritually hollow. The item works. It doesn't *carry*. A commission built with full presence, genuine care, or creative excitement produces something that carries that forward in a way that's felt and practically real. Builders therefore train their emotional discipline as seriously as their craft. The best ones aren't just skilled — they are *settled*. **A Builder cannot hide their feelings. A Builder's craft cannot lie.**

**The internal tension (flavour, not faction split):** Profit versus quality. Some Builders move volume — quick commissions, technically correct, emotionally neutral, commercially viable. Others spend weeks or months on a single piece. Both approaches coexist in the same workshop. The argument never resolves. It just runs underneath everything, permanently.

**Rank ladder:**
- D · Untempered — standard catalogue items, no customisation
- C · Drawn — tuned items, refined to your specific Spark style
- B · Set — composite items, two properties from your Spark domain
- A · Proven — named items: a proper name, a specific function, something built for you that didn't exist before
- S · **Skyhammer** — experimental commissions; the Weld asks *you* what they should try to make. Collaboration, not catalogue.

**Standing mechanic:** No decay. Standing moves only when you act — complete missions for the Weld, gain favour, unlock higher FOCI access. Neglect them and you stay where you are, which in a city where foci are rare, means you fall behind.

**Crisis — The Hard Close:** Harm a Weld member, damage or steal a commissioned item, publicly humiliate a Builder's work, use a Weld-made item against the Weld itself. The doors close. No warning, no gradual erosion — one act, one consequence. The only foci workshop in Sora, barred. Getting back in, if it is even possible, costs something significant. The Weld does not forget, because their work doesn't.

**Named figure — Fae (The Leader)**

Nineteen years old. The youngest faction leader in Sora's recorded history, and the only one whose appointment produced no argument — because anyone who watched her work knew before they finished watching.

*Spark:* Creation — one of the rarest inclinations in the world. A lean toward the fundamental law of bringing-into-being rather than toward any natural force. Most Sparks push toward something the world already contains. Hers leans toward the act of making itself.

*Spirit:* The All-Seeing. Her manifestation went toward perception rather than production: she sees the connections between materials — specifically, how they want to come together for *this client's* Spark. Not what she would make, not what the catalogue offers — what this person's power needs in order to express correctly. Two people with identical commissions would receive different foci from Fae, because she's reading the person as much as the brief.

*In practice:* She's the most sought-after builder in the world. The Weld's waiting list extends four months. She takes from it when she decides to. Money gets you a place on the list and exceptional craft — her sight is always active, the result always correct. Earning her commission is different. It requires her to fully understand you, and that isn't guaranteed by standing alone.

*The Kure State:* When inspiration strikes, or when she reaches complete understanding of a client, something unlocks. The workshop has a name for it borrowed from the faith — *Kure's hand* — the same way any city names things that feel too large for ordinary language. Nobody in the Weld knows why they chose Kure's name specifically. It just felt right. The room goes still in the way it only goes still when she's fully in it. Those who've witnessed it describe warmth that isn't temperature, the feeling of things settling into shapes they were always supposed to occupy. In this state she can make anything she has the material and the intent for. What she produces carries something that even the best standard commissions don't. You cannot pay for the state to manifest. You can only make yourself worth fully understanding, and then wait to see what she sees.

*Note (design doc only — Buried Irony):* The Kure state is functionally the ISN sequence (Intent-Structure-Name from the Amatsukōbō's founding methodology) operating at full capacity through a direct lineage descendant. The folk-borrowing of Kure's name is accidentally accurate: she is the Amatsukōbō tradition reaching its peak expression two thousand years on. Neither she nor the Weld knows any of this. The name survived as feeling, not history.

*[Filter check: "Kure's hand" as workshop slang is valid — Kure IS a named saint in Ryuugenism, the city already knows the name in religious context. The connection to the founding lineage stays in this doc. Fae's age (19) earns its weight through capability, not exception — FONT rewards what you can do, not how long you've been doing it. Named Rival slot: she is the natural Weld milestone-10 figure — the commission she personally accepts, with the chance of the state manifesting.]*

### 4.4 — THE KINDLED *(fleshed)* ✨

**What it is:** The largest and most diverse faction of fighters in Sora. Not a guild, not a faith, not a political body by design — and yet the one faction trusted to speak for the people of Sora. They occupy the **Star Path**, a dojo in the south of the outer ring, and they believe one thing absolutely: your Spark is a direction, and combat is how you walk it. Every fight is a step. Every step is toward something you have not yet become.

The Kindled do not face the dungeon. They face each other — in sparring, in monthly tournaments, and once a year in the Grand Tournament, the loudest, most public event in Sora's calendar. The path to power here is not depth. It is expression. To be Kindled at the ceiling is to have shown your spirit in a way nobody has seen before, in front of everyone, and made them feel it.

**The philosophy — and where Titan fits into it:** The Kindled believe in their own power. Only that. Your Spark, your spirit, your expression — unmediated, unaugmented, earned through the friction of real combat against real opponents. This is not a rule posted on a wall. It is the texture of everything the faction does, and it puts them in direct and permanent ideological tension with Titan.

The Kindled do not ban Titan fighters from competing. They *invite* them — because the tournament is open to all, and because for a long time the outcome of that invitation confirmed everything the Kindled believed. A faction built on shortcuts, on artificial power grafted over the gap where a Spark should reach — how could they match a fighter fully attuned to their own? The Kindled watched Titan fighters compete and saw proof.

That was before Nyx.

The win rate is approaching 50/50 now, faction-wide. The Kindled have an answer for this — they always do, because Sora adapts, and the Kindled are Soran to their core. But the answer is not yet clear. What a Titan victory in the Grand Tournament would *mean* — to the philosophy, to the path, to everything the faction holds self-evident — is a question the Star Path dojo is not ready to answer. It is sitting in the room at every sparring session, unspoken. Sora has always adapted to what challenges it. Whether the Kindled *lead* that adaptation or are pulled into it is still being decided.

**The Grand Tournament:** Monthly sparring keeps the faction sharp and the standing ladder moving. The Grand Tournament — held once a year — is something else entirely. The whole city comes. Not just fighters, not just the faithful or the Guild-contracted or the Titan-sponsored: *everyone*. It is Sora's shared spectacle, the one event where the inner ring and the outer ring are in the same crowd. The fighter who wins it becomes the **Champion** — a title with no administrative function and total cultural weight. You cannot buy a Champion's standing. You cannot inherit it. You win it in front of the city, and the city decides what it means.

**The Champion — Kuregar:** Born in the inner ring. Fought his way to the Star Path the way most Kindled fighters do — not with connections, not with resources, with the friction of accumulating wins against people who were trying to stop him. Reigning Champion for two years. Currently undefeated.

His Spark leans toward foresight — the same fundamental law as the Veneration's founding prophet, expressed in an entirely different direction. Where Leona's Foreseen moved toward divination and prophecy, Kuregar's spirit, **The King's Sight**, moves toward combat. One second. That is what it gives him — a window one second ahead, a felt sense of where the strike is going before it arrives, where the weight is shifting before it shifts. His foci, **The First Ruler**, lets him channel the King's Sight directly through his weapon. His opponents describe the sensation of fighting him as trying to hit a thing that already knows it won't be there. The weapon finds you. You do not find it.

He is one of the strongest fighters in Sora right now. The city knows it. He knows it. He carries that without performance — the inner ring does not produce people who need to announce what they are.

Two years in, Nyx has fought him twice and lost both times. In their third meeting she landed a blow. It was one contact, in a losing fight. But Kuregar sees one second ahead, and she touched him anyway — which means she did something in that moment that slipped outside the window. Both of them know what that means. The fourth fight will be different from all three that came before it.

**The Sages — merit made visible:** The Kindled hold one of the five faction seats on the Celestial Assembly's proposal floor. That seat is filled by one of the **Three Sages**, elected by all of Sora — not just Kindled members, every Soran with a voice in the city's governance. The candidates are always Kindled. The voters are everyone.

This sounds like an advantage until you understand how Sora actually votes. The city values merit above almost everything else, and merit in Sora is demonstrated publicly. A Kindled fighter has a natural edge — their record is visible, their expression is watched, the city has seen what they can do in the ring. But a Cult Abyssal who has gone deeper than anyone this year, or a Venerator whose wisdom has held a district together through a crisis, carries a different kind of demonstrated merit, and Sora recognises all of it. The Kindled's advantage is presence, not lock. The best candidate tends to win regardless of faction. That the seat is often Kindled says something about how well the Star Path produces people worth trusting. Whether that trust is well-placed is a question Sora does not ask out loud — it simply watches.

The Sages represent the inner ring specifically, and loudly. The Kindled are rooted there — the fighters who came up from proximity to Mugen, who know what it costs to live closest to the thing everyone is chasing. That advocacy is genuine. It also keeps the faction anchored to the part of the city that has the least and wants the most, which is a different kind of power than the Guild's administration or the faith's devotion.

Champion and Sage are separate. Some fighters stay in the ring — the path of pure expression, uninterrupted. Others choose the wider responsibility. Both are respected. The faction does not weight one above the other, because the Kindled understand that fighting well and governing well are different disciplines, and Sora needs people who are honest about which one they are.

**Rank ladder — Kindled → Honed → Forged → Blaze → Ascended:**

Every rank is a stage of expression. You enter as **Kindled** — lit, unrefined, the direction present but the shape not yet found. **Honed** is the first real sharpening: combat has started to tell you something about what your Spark actually is. **Forged** is sustained pressure applied consistently — you have been tested repeatedly and are still standing, and the standing has changed you. **Blaze** is someone whose expression already stops the room; people come to watch *them* specifically, not just the match. **Ascended** is the ceiling: your spirit expressed in a manner so uniquely your own that those who witness it feel something they cannot explain. Not the strongest. The most fully themselves.

Rank moves through standing — earned in sparring, in monthly tournaments, in the Grand Tournament, in faction missions. No shortcuts. No fast-tracks. The Star Path does not have a back door.

**Standing track: ARDOUR.** What builds in a Kindled fighter across a career — the accumulated heat of a life spent in honest combat, expressed and witnessed. It does not decay from inaction the way some standing tracks erode. It simply does not move unless you fight. In a city where the Kindled's merit is built in public, standing still is its own kind of loss.

**Crisis — The Dimming:** Compete dishonourably. Use methods that mask your Spark rather than express it — fight in a way that obscures what you are rather than revealing it. The Star Path reads this not as rule-breaking but as a betrayal of the only thing the Kindled actually ask: *be what you are, in front of everyone.* A fighter who dims their own expression forfeits their standing. Getting it back means proving, publicly, that the dimming was a moment and not a direction.

## IDENTITY REFRAME — BUILD SPEC (agreed, not yet built into app)
*Replaces the old creator's rigid Archetype step + compulsory cape persona layer. Astroprisma model: constrained character DEFINITION (preset dropdowns), free world INTERACTION (behaviour never boxed).*
- **Creator flow becomes:** Personality → Awakening → Spirit → Class → **Prompt A** → **Prompt B** → (optional **Face toggle**).

Carries MORAL COLOUR implicitly (e.g. Good / Evil / Justice / No Compromise / Devotion-style values) — replaces the 4 archetypes; the game never *declares* you hero/villain, your B-self + behaviour reveal it.

- **Face toggle (optional, OFF by default):** "Do you put on a face when you fight?" If ON → persona name/look fields (metaphorical mask, or literal by choice). If OFF → creator never mentions costumes. This is where cape flair lives for those who want it; absent for those who don't.
- **Divine flavour** woven across Personality/Awakening/Class/Prompt-B options per The Faith above.
- ⚠️ Cuts the compulsory superhero suit/codename layer Kyle flagged as bolted-on.
- **Spark** = single power-level number; gates spirits held + Luck pool. ALSO the spiritual heart/nervous-system that houses a spirit. A *direction* (inclination toward one law of the universe), never a hardcoded type.
- Spirits: **power given form**, Spark manifested, with ego/will. Creativity = expressiveness of manifestation. Fighting-type (loud, swagger) vs non-fighting (dry, cutting). Voice mirrors who the person *truly* is, sometimes before they know it.
- **Universality (MHA-quirk model):** everyone has a spirit; most are MUNDANE. Only a few have the training/desire/capacity for hero/villain/anti-hero/antagonist play. This is why Sora is a city, not a war zone.
- **Spark geography:** strongest combatant-tier Sparks are BORN in Sora (thick bloodline descent from the original will-workers). Other settlements get strong Sparks rarely + unreliably. This imbalance is the engine of the wider world's need for Sora.
- **Two lineages, one descent:** will-magic descended into the SPARK (born talent) and into CRAFT/TECH (the augmented/forged line — the world's workaround for being born without the river). Same root, two branches. NOT rival systems.
- **Tech level:** lived-in mid-tech (Boruto-grade — works, woven through daily life; past medieval, short of modern). PLUS reclaimed relic-tech from the deep that outclasses modern craft (Made-in-Abyss flavour) = the unreachable wonder. *(References = calibration only, never in-game.)*

- **Breaks:** negative (hardened/dominating-next-spirit/trust-thinned-but-latent) or positive (mutual/sacrificial-grief-protective). A break CAN catch and repair in the instant — rare, costly, the glory-ceiling moment.
- ⚠️ **Bonded people CANNOT sense each other on the street.** Only in-dungeon: a pulling pressure in the core senses loose manifestations. (Beat pools need scrubbing — see 1.5.)
- Bond moods: **steady / frayed / hostile**. Restore at morning.
- **The Bleed** = narrative/thematic device, not a power. Two lives in one city inevitably collide; collisions thicken toward an arc's climax.
- **The Guild** = loose jobs-and-classification network; sanctions power use, doesn't define you as hero/villain. Identity is free (Prompt A/B + behaviour), NOT a chosen archetype. *(Superseded the old "player chooses archetype" line.)*
